redesign

  • Jump to Content
  1. Increased Kohl’s logo sizeRemoving the secondary horizontal nav and reducing the banner height allows for dramatically enlarging the Kohl’s logo, providing increased awareness of the brand.
  2. Much more white spaceRenders the page easier on the eyes, and each element more findable. Made possible by getting rid of the horizontal nav that used to sit right underneath the logo and search box—all of its links can be relocated elsewhere.
  3. Moved search box to right side of pageNot only is this placement more conventional, the search box is no longer crowding the Kohl’s logo.
  4. Increased prominence of global navExtra space in the new layout allows for a bigger font in the global nav. In combination with the reduced banner height below, this makes it easier to see and use.
  5. Reduced height of bannerBy refining each section of the banner to its core message, the global nav stands out more, and the banner's messages would come through more clearly.
    • For example: Free Shipping / with $75 purchase / details
  6. Vertical nav on leftThat's the conventional location, at least for audiences that mostly read (and scan web pages) left to right.
  7. Nav further up the pagePlacing this nav in the expected spot, and giving it a clearer identity, eliminates the need for a label (More to Explore). Now more of it is initially visible on smaller displays.
  8. Simpler content areaNo duplicate content, and limit the number of promotions to avoid visual overload.

  1. Search box is crowding the logo; tagline is blending in with the horizontal nav directly below.
  2. Two horizontal nav bars stacked on top of each other creates uncertainty as to which is primary: the one with the larger font, or the one placed higher on the page?
    • This arrangement also makes for too many links in a small space; it is difficult to scan and keep track of all of the navigation options.
  3. Of course, that the upper navigation bar has no theme precludes the possibility of it being logically related to other page elements.
    • The links from this navigation bar can more effectively be integrated into other areas of the page, and remain at least as visible to the user.
  4. The banner dwarfs the main navigation, and there’s too much info for a such a small space, contributing to the busy feel of the page. Too much detail is provided up front; the “details” link at the bottom of each section is all that is needed.
  5. Duplicate content, stacked right on top of each other.
  6. This nav is labeled More to Explore because otherwise users would probably wouldn't know what it is, due to its lack of a clear identity and odd placement.
  7. The categories in this nav don't begin until about a third of the way down the content area.
